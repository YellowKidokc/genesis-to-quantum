Physical — Newton

$$F = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}$$
  • $F$ — gravitational force (the pull between two objects)
  • $G$ — Newton's gravitational constant
  • $m_1, m_2$ — masses of the two objects
  • $r^2$ — distance between them, squared

Two objects pull on each other with a force proportional to how much of them there is and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Double the distance, the force drops to a quarter. But no matter how far apart they get, the pull never reaches zero. Gravity is universal and inescapable.

Here's what quantum mechanics actually says: before you measure a system, it exists in a superposition of all possible states simultaneously. Not "we don't know which state it's in" — that would be classical uncertainty. The system IS in all states at once. When you measure, the wavefunction collapses to one outcome. The Born Rule tells you the probability.

That collapse — the reduction from all-possible to one-actual — is what measurement does. And measurement requires the right kind of engagement with the system.

Physical — Quantum Mechanics

Schrodinger Equation

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\Psi\rangle = \hat{H}|\Psi\rangle$$
  • $|\Psi\rangle$ — wavefunction (full quantum state)
  • $\hat{H}$ — Hamiltonian (energy operator)
  • $\hbar$ — reduced Planck constant

A quantum system evolves smoothly in superposition. All possibilities coexist. The equation is deterministic — but the future state is a superposition, not a definite outcome.

Born Rule

$$P(x) = |\langle x|\Psi\rangle|^2$$

When you measure, the probability of getting outcome $x$ is the square of the overlap between the current state and the measured state. The measurement collapses the superposition.
