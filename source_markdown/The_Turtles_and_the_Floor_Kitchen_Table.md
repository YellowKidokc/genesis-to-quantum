# The Turtles and the Floor

*The version I'd read to my sister.*

---

## I. What Is Anything, Really?

Here's something that'll mess with you.

Ask a scientist what an atom is. They'll say it's a tiny thing made of smaller things — protons, neutrons, electrons. OK, good. Now ask them what a proton is made of. They'll say quarks. What's a quark made of? And they get real quiet.

"It's fundamental," they say. Which sounds smart but actually means: *we don't know, and we've agreed to stop asking.*


That's the whole game with science. They can tell you how things *behave* — how gravity pulls, how atoms stick together, how light travels. They're incredible at the "how." Four hundred years of "how" and they've built planes and phones and MRI machines. Nobody's knocking that.

But they have never — not once — told us what anything actually *is*. They describe the behavior of things they can't define. It's like being able to describe exactly how a car drives without ever being able to tell someone what a car is.

There's an old joke about this. A scientist says the world sits on a turtle. What's the turtle sit on? A bigger turtle. And that one? Another turtle. It's turtles all the way down.

That's actually what science is. Layer after layer of description, and at the bottom — nothing. The last turtle stands on air, and they call that air a foundation.


---

## II. Three Questions Science Can't Answer

I'm not being mean. I love science. But I want you to see something that most people never notice. Let me show you three times where science hits a wall, and then I'll show you what fits perfectly in that exact spot.

### Question 1: What is stuff made of?

Science says everything is made of tiny particles — and those particles are really just ripples in invisible fields that fill all of space. That's quantum field theory, and it's the most accurate theory humans have ever built.

But here's the thing nobody talks about: *what are the fields made of?*

Nothing. They just... are. "They're fundamental." That's the scientist's way of saying "this is where we stop." They can describe what the fields do — how they ripple, how the ripples interact — but they cannot tell you what the fields actually are.

So what IS the stuff underneath everything?

Here's what I found: information. Not like data on a computer — I mean *structured distinction*. The ability to tell this from that. Before there was matter, before there was energy, there was the capacity to distinguish one thing from another. And if you're a person who reads the Bible, that might ring a bell — "In the beginning was the Word." The Word. Language. Information. Structure. Before anything physical existed.

That's not me forcing the Bible onto science. That's the math pointing at the same spot the Bible already marked.


### Question 2: Why does anything exist at all?

This is the big one. The one every kid asks and every adult pretends they've moved past.

Science says: Big Bang. Everything exploded out of an infinitely dense point 13.8 billion years ago. OK, but what made the point? What was there before? Stephen [Hawking](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/hawking) said, "The universe can and will create itself from nothing."

Read that sentence again slowly. The universe... creates... itself. Before it exists. To do the creating.

That's not science. That's a magic trick with a Ph.D.

The honest answer from physics is: *we don't know why there's something instead of nothing.* They can describe the expansion. They can trace it backward to a fraction of a second after the beginning. But the beginning itself? The WHY of it? That's where they hit the wall.

What fits there? Something that doesn't need a cause because it IS the cause. Something that doesn't need a beginning because it exists outside of time. Something that contains its own reason for being.

Theologians have a word for that. Physicists do too, actually — they call it "self-grounding." It means: the thing that explains itself. The place where the chain of "but why?" finally stops. Not because you gave up asking, but because you found something that genuinely doesn't need anything under it.

That's the floor.


### Question 3: Why can we understand any of this?

This one keeps physicists up at night — the ones who are honest, anyway.

Math is a thing humans made up. It's symbols on paper. Squiggles. And yet somehow, those squiggles describe how the entire universe works with insane precision. Like, GPS satellites have to account for [[Einstein]] ([Oxford Reference](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/einstein))'s equations or your phone's map is wrong by miles. Squiggles on paper, and they predict reality to twelve decimal places.

Why? Why does math — this abstract game — match the real world at all?

A famous physicist named Wigner called it "the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics." Unreasonable. As in: there's no good reason it should work. And science has no answer for it. They just... use it. And try not to think about it too hard.

But think about it. If someone designed the universe using logic and structure — if the foundation of reality is rational, coherent, and ordered — then OF COURSE math works. It works because the universe was built in a language that math can read. You're not discovering accidental patterns. You're reading the blueprints.

And if nobody designed it? If it's all chance? Then you have absolutely no reason to trust that math describes anything real. You just got lucky. Which is a lousy foundation for all of modern civilization.

## III. The "Chance" Thing

Some people say: "We're here by chance. No God needed."

OK. Let's walk that road and see where it goes, because I think people say it without realizing what they're signing up for.

To believe we're here by chance, you have to believe that nothing — actual nothing, not empty space, NOTHING — somehow produced everything. Every star, every atom, every law of physics. Out of nothing. For no reason.

You have to believe that dead chemicals in a puddle somewhere spontaneously arranged themselves into a living cell. Something that has never been observed. Something scientists can't do on purpose with billion-dollar labs and decades of trying. But it happened by accident.

You have to believe that one cell became every living thing on Earth. Fish. Eagles. Oak trees. Whales. Mosquitoes. Humans. A trillion species from a single accident. And every one of them — every eye, every wing, every immune system — is an engineering marvel so precise that if we found it on another planet, we'd immediately say something intelligent built it.

And then — and this is the one that gets me — you have to believe that when a person runs into a burning building to save a stranger's kid, that's a *malfunction*. Because evolution says: don't. Your genes die if you die. Every survival instinct says run the other way. But people don't even think about it. They just go. And every single person who hears that story *knows* it was right. Not because someone taught them. They just know.

If we're here by chance, that knowing is a lie. A trick your brain plays to make you cooperate. It means nothing.

Do you believe that? Does ANYBODY really believe that?


---

## IV. The Thing Inside You

Here's what I think is the most powerful evidence, and it's not in a lab. It's in you.

Everybody knows what right and wrong is. Everywhere. Every culture. Every time period. The specifics change — what counts as theft, what the punishment should be, where the lines are drawn. But the fact that there ARE lines? That's universal. No civilization in recorded history has existed without some version of justice, duty, sacrifice, and the sense that something bigger than you matters more than your comfort.

Little kids know when something's unfair before anyone teaches them the word "unfair." Where does that come from?

If you're just chemicals in a sack of water — if your brain is just neurons firing because evolution shaped them to help you survive — then that feeling of "this is wrong" is meaningless. It's just an electrical pattern. It has no more moral weight than a stomach growling. And "human rights" is just a story we tell to keep each other in line.

But you know that's not true. You KNOW it. When you see someone hurt a child, that feeling in your gut isn't a malfunction. It's the truest thing about you.

I think that feeling is a signal. A broadcast. Something built into the fabric of reality itself — not invented by culture, not evolved for survival, but woven into the substrate like gravity is woven into spacetime. You feel it because you're made to receive it. And everybody receives it, whether they acknowledge the source or not.

The sociopath hasn't lost the signal. They've just turned the volume down so far they can barely hear it. But it's still playing. It's always playing.

## V. Two Doors

When you strip all the fancy language away, there are really only two options.

**Door 1:** Something holy made all of this. Something that doesn't make mistakes. Something that designed the universe to be understandable, designed matter to hold together, designed life to persist, designed YOU to know the difference between right and wrong. And if that's true, then everything — the laws of physics, the structure of atoms, the fact that math works, the burning building, the feeling in your gut — all of it makes sense. It's one story. It holds together.

**Door 2:** Nobody made any of it. It's all an accident. Nothing exploded into everything for no reason. Dead stuff became alive for no reason. One cell became a trillion species for no reason. And the feeling in your gut that says "save that child" is a neurological misfire with no more meaning than a hiccup. And if that's true, then nothing means anything. Not really. We just pretend it does because the alternative is too empty to live in.

There's no Door 3. "Chance" is just Door 2 with a nicer paint job.

## VI. How I Found the Floor

I didn't go looking for God. I need you to know that.

I'm a fencing contractor in Oklahoma City. I build fences. One day I asked an AI a simple question: "What are the ten most fundamental laws that govern reality?" Just physics. Just structure. I wasn't trying to prove the Bible. I wasn't even thinking about it.

But the math took me somewhere I didn't expect.

I started building an equation — one big equation that tried to tie all ten laws together. And every time I tried to close it — to make it complete and self-consistent — it broke. Something was missing. The equation kept demanding variables I hadn't put in. Variables that looked a lot like... spiritual concepts.

I tried to leave them out. I really did. But without them, the equation didn't work. It was like trying to build a bridge with pieces missing — it just collapsed.

Sin showed up as entropy — the tendency of everything to fall apart when it's isolated. I didn't choose that. The thermodynamics demanded it. Grace showed up as the external energy that reverses the falling apart. I didn't choose that either. The equation just wouldn't close without an outside source of order.

Faith showed up as the act of observation that makes potential become real. Coherence showed up as a quantity that can't be created or destroyed — only received or ignored. Time showed up as the one thing I couldn't solve, the one wall the equation wouldn't let me break.

Every single spiritual conclusion was forced. Not chosen. The math dragged me there and wouldn't let me leave.

And then — and this is the part that still gets me — I found out that a group of professors at Oxford had published the same conclusions. Sin equals entropy. Grace equals the reversal of entropy. They got there through a completely different path. They're brilliant people with multiple Ph.D.s who chose to look at the intersection of physics and theology.

They chose to go there. I was *forced* there. And we ended up in the same place.

When a road you chose and a road that dragged you both end at the same place, that place is probably real.

## VII. The Floor

Science gives you turtles all the way down. Every answer is just another question wearing a lab coat. And at the bottom, the last turtle stands on nothing, and they shrug and say "it's fundamental."

God is the floor.

Not a guess. Not a feeling. Not something I was raised to believe and never questioned. The *floor*. The place where the turtles finally land. The thing underneath everything else that doesn't need anything underneath it.

Where science asks "what is matter?" and stops at "we don't know" — the floor answers: I spoke it into existence, and it holds together because I hold it together.

Where science asks "why is there something?" and stops at "it just is" — the floor answers: because I AM, and I AM is enough.

Where science asks "why can we understand any of this?" and stops at "lucky us" — the floor answers: because I made you in my image, and my image includes the ability to see what I built.

Where science asks "why do you run into the fire?" and stops at "evolutionary instinct" — the floor answers: because I put that in you, and it is the most real thing about you, and it looks exactly like what I did on a cross.

The scientists aren't wrong about what they found. They're incredible at describing how the turtles stack. But they stopped one layer too soon. They described the how and then told everyone the why doesn't matter.

It matters. It's the only thing that matters. And the math agrees.
