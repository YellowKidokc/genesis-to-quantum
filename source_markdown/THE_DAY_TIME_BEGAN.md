## II. The [[Schrödinger]] ([Oxford Reference](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/schr%C3%B6dinger)) Equation Already Knew This

Here's what should have told us this from the start — except the framework wasn't asking the right question yet.

Quantum mechanics has two dynamical rules. The [[Schrödinger]] ([Oxford Reference](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/schr%C3%B6dinger)) equation governs evolution between measurements:

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial|\Psi\rangle}{\partial t} = \hat{H}|\Psi\rangle$$

Where:
- **i** = the imaginary unit — signals that this is complex-valued evolution, not classical decay
- **ℏ** = the reduced Planck constant — the scale of quantum action
- **∂|Ψ⟩/∂t** = how fast the quantum state changes — the rate of evolution
- **Ĥ** = the Hamiltonian — the energy operator, what drives the evolution

In plain English: *This equation describes how a quantum system changes over time when no one is measuring it. It runs forward. It also runs perfectly backward. Neither direction is preferred. The physics is completely symmetric between past and future.*

Do you see how this connects? The [[Schrödinger]] ([Oxford Reference](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/schr%C3%B6dinger)) equation is Eden physics. The six days of Genesis 1 are [[Schrödinger]] ([Oxford Reference](https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/schr%C3%B6dinger)) evolution: ordered, sequential, purposeful — but time-symmetric. God could, in principle, run it backward. Nothing has been made irreversible yet. The sequence is real. The arrow is not.

Then the measurement postulate: when observation occurs, the wavefunction collapses. Irreversibly. The specific outcome does not un-happen. The superposition does not return. The system cannot be put back the way it was.

This is the second rule of quantum mechanics. Physicists have been using both rules simultaneously for a hundred years. Nobody has fully explained why there are two. Nobody has derived the second rule from the first. They are separate. They describe different regimes.
