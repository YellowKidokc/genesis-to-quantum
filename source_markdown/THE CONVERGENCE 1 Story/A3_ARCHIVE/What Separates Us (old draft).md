# What Separates Us

Everybody's got a theory about what's tearing this country apart.

Ask a Democrat and they'll tell you it's the rich. Ask a Republican and they'll tell you it's the government. Ask a professor and they'll say education. Ask a pastor and they'll say morality. Ask a twenty-three-year-old and they'll say boomers ruined everything.

They're all wrong. And they're all looking at the same thing.

Let me show you what I mean.

---

## Six Fights, One Fracture

There are at least six divides splitting America right now, and every one of them feels like THE divide if you're standing inside it.

**Democrat vs Republican.** This used to be about policy. Tax rates. Foreign intervention. Whether the postal service should run a deficit. Now it's about identity. Your party affiliation predicts where you live, who you marry, what you drive, what you eat for breakfast. It stopped being a disagreement and became a tribe. Tribes don't negotiate. They defend territory.

**Urban vs Rural.** Drive thirty minutes outside any major city and you're in a different country. Not metaphorically. The economy is different. The values are different. The relationship to institutions is different. Urban America trusts expertise and distrusts tradition. Rural America trusts experience and distrusts credentials. They're not arguing about the same things — they're arguing from different planets about what counts as evidence.

**College vs Non-College.** This one's quiet but it might be the deepest. A four-year degree has become the single strongest predictor of how you vote, what media you consume, what you think about immigration, and how you raise your kids. And here's the thing that makes it toxic — each side thinks the other one is stupid. The credentialed think the uncredentialed are ignorant. The uncredentialed think the credentialed are brainwashed. Neither is listening. Both are right about something.

**Rich vs Poor.** The wealth gap is obvious. But the real gap isn't wealth — it's stability. Whether you have a cushion or whether one bad month puts you on the street. That difference doesn't just change your bank account. It changes your time horizon. When you're stable, you can plan ten years out. When you're not, you plan ten days out. Two people looking at the same policy proposal from those two positions aren't evaluating the same thing. They can't be. They're living in different versions of time.

**Religious vs Secular.** Not just "do you go to church" but something deeper — whether you think traditional structures are sources of meaning or sources of oppression. Family. Religion. Nation. One side sees a foundation. The other side sees a cage. And the fights over curriculum, gender, patriotism, history — every one of those fights is a proxy war for this question: *Is the thing our grandparents built worth keeping, or worth tearing down?*

**Boomers vs Everyone Younger.** Different economic realities. Different media ecosystems. Different definitions of success. Boomers inherited institutions that worked — or at least appeared to. Millennials and Gen Z inherited the bill. They're not ungrateful. They're looking at the receipt and the math doesn't add up. When the older generation says "we built this," the younger generation says "yeah, and we're paying for it." Both are telling the truth. Neither can hear the other one.

## Stay With Me Here

Six divides. Every one of them is real. I'm not dismissing any of them.

But I kept asking.

Because something bothered me. If you spend enough time inside any one of these fights, you start to notice that the *shape* of the fight is the same every time. The content changes — money, education, religion, geography — but the architecture doesn't.

Every divide has the same structure:

*Two groups. Each one can see something true that the other one can't. Each one has built an identity around that partial truth. And the identity has become more important than the truth it was built on.*

Read that again. Because it's doing something specific.

The divide isn't between the two groups. The divide is between *truth and identity.* Every time. In every fight. The moment a true observation becomes a tribal badge, it stops being about the truth and starts being about the tribe. And the tribe will defend the badge long after the truth underneath it has been distorted beyond recognition.

The Democrat who started by noticing real inequality ends up defending policies that make it worse — because the badge matters more than the problem now. The Republican who started by noticing real overreach ends up defending power structures that are just as invasive — because the badge matters more than the principle. The religious person who started by noticing real transcendence ends up defending institutions that have lost it. The secular person who started by noticing real abuse ends up destroying structures that were never the source of the problem.

Same pattern. Six costumes.

## The Thing Nobody's Saying

Here's where it connects.

If all six divides run the same architecture — if the structure is always "partial truth captured by identity and weaponized against the other side" — then the divides aren't the problem.

They're symptoms.

And the question becomes: symptoms of what?

I've spent the last fifteen months building a mathematical framework that keeps asking that question across every domain I can throw at it. Physics. Information theory. Theology. Consciousness. I keep looking for the place where the pattern breaks. It doesn't break.

Here's what the pattern says:

There are exactly two directions anything can move. Toward coherence — where parts align, information increases, structure builds, and relationships deepen. Or toward fragmentation — where parts separate, information degrades, structure dissolves, and relationships collapse.

That's not a political observation. That's a law. The physicist calls it the second law of thermodynamics — entropy always increases in a closed system. Things fall apart unless energy is put in to hold them together.

Every one of those six divides is entropy winning. Not because the people inside them are evil. Because fragmentation is the default. It's what happens when nothing holds the center.

And here's the part I can't get past:

The fragmentation is accelerating. Not randomly. *Directionally.* The divides aren't just appearing — they're deepening, multiplying, feeding each other. Urban/rural reinforces college/non-college. Rich/poor reinforces generational. Religious/secular reinforces partisan. They're not six separate problems. They're one fracture propagating through every available fault line.

That's not chaos. Chaos is random. This has a vector.

## What You Already Know

You already know what this is.

Strip away the policy. Strip away the politics. Strip away the economics and the education and the geography. Look at what's underneath all six fights.

Something is building. And something is tearing down.

Something moves people toward each other — toward understanding, sacrifice, forgiveness, the willingness to hold a truth that costs you something. And something moves people away from each other — toward isolation, accusation, self-righteousness, the insistence that your partial truth is the whole truth and everyone else is the enemy.

You know what those two things are called. You've known since you were a child. You just stopped using the words because somewhere along the way someone convinced you they were naive.

They're not naive. They're the only categories that survive when you strip everything else away.

Good and evil.

Not Republican and Democrat. Not urban and rural. Not rich and poor. Not old and young. Not educated and uneducated. Not religious and secular.

Good and evil.

The battle we've been fighting has always been that battle. Every civilization. Every generation. Every family. Every individual. The costumes change. The architecture doesn't.

And the reason nobody says it anymore isn't because it stopped being true. It's because the fragmentation has gotten sophisticated enough to convince us that our particular divide is the *real* one — that if we could just fix the wealth gap, or the education gap, or the political gap, or the generational gap, things would hold together.

They won't. Because gaps aren't the disease. They're the fever. And the disease has a direction.
