## Why We Reach for the Badge

But there's something underneath the pattern that's worse than the pattern itself.

Ask yourself: when you judge someone on the other side of one of those divides, what are you judging them on?

It's almost always something you're good at.

The educated person judges the uneducated person on knowledge — because knowledge is where they succeeded. The financially stable person judges the struggling person on discipline — because discipline is where they kept score. The religious person judges the secular person on morality — because morality is the arena where they've put in the work.

Nobody judges on the thing they're worst at. Nobody walks into the arena where they lose and picks that as the measuring stick. You pick the arena where you win. And you hold it up like it's the only arena that matters.

That's not righteous anger. That's self-protection dressed up as discernment.

And here's what makes it poison: the more you do it, the more you see. Judge someone on their politics and suddenly everything they do confirms your judgment. Their car. Their neighborhood. Their vocabulary. Their haircut. The filter narrows. The contempt deepens. And you stop seeing a person and start seeing a category.

That's not six inches from dehumanization. That's the same road. In wartime they give the enemy a name that isn't human so you can do the unthinkable. We're doing it now with words. With eye-rolls. With algorithms that feed you the worst version of the other side until you can't imagine they're people who love their kids and worry about their mortgage and lie awake at night wondering if they're getting it right.

The feedback loop is the trap. Accusation breeds accusation. Contempt breeds contempt. And every cycle, the system closes a little more. Becomes a little more airtight. A little harder to crack open.

So what cracks it?

Not a rule. Not "stop judging." That's just another rule, and rules don't break feedback loops. They just add a new layer of guilt on top of the existing contempt.

What cracks it is the thing that was supposed to come first.

Gratitude.

Not gratitude as a feeling. Gratitude as a *posture.* The decision — before the accusation, before the finger-pointing, before the tribal badge goes on — to start with what you've been given instead of what the other person owes you.

The physicist would call it an initial condition. The information theorist would call it a prior. The theologian would call it something older.

It's the one move that breaks the closed system. Because gratitude doesn't come from inside. You can't generate it from nothing. It requires recognizing that something was given to you that you didn't earn. And the moment you recognize that, the finger that was pointing outward has to come back.

Not to punish yourself. To see clearly. To measure yourself by the same standard you were about to use on someone else.

That's the hardest thing a human being can do. And it's the only thing that works.
