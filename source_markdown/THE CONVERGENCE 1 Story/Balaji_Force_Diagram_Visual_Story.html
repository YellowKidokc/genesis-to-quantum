Part 03

The Debt Singularity: When Money Loses Meaning

Balaji's sharpest economic argument: technology is deflationary — it makes things cheaper, better, faster. But government spending is inflationary — it pumps money into the system, raising prices. These two forces have been at war for decades, and the government has been winning.

Inflation vs. Deflation: The Hidden War
Technology pushes prices down. Government spending pushes prices up. The gap is the tax you don't see.

The result: things that technology touches get cheaper (TVs, computers, software). Things that government touches get more expensive (healthcare, education, housing). The Cantillon Effect — those closest to new money benefit first; those farthest away get the inflation without the windfall.

Government prints money to fund deficit spending
↓
New money enters through banks, government contractors, federal employees
↓
Asset prices rise first (stocks, real estate) — benefiting the already-wealthy
↓
Consumer prices rise last — hitting wages, groceries, rent
↓
Working class feels the squeeze: real wages stagnate while cost of living climbs
↓
Rich/poor divide deepens — not from greed, but from monetary mechanics
Convergence Bridge

In the Convergence series, we showed the rich/poor divide isn't really about wealth — it's about stability. The Cantillon Effect is the mechanism. It explains why one group plans ten years out while the other plans ten days out. It's not a character difference. It's a structural entropy gradient — those closer to the money source experience less monetary noise; those farther away drown in it. The equation dχ/dt ∝ −S + G expressed in dollar signs.
