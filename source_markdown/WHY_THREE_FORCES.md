# Why Three Forces

There are four fundamental forces in nature. Gravity, electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force. Every physics student learns this in their first semester.

But there's a question underneath that list that nobody teaches in first semester: why those four? Why not three? Why not seven? Why does the universe have exactly four forces with exactly those properties?

Gravity stands apart — it's geometric, described by the curvature of spacetime itself. Set it aside for now. The other three forces — strong, weak, and electromagnetic — are described by a single mathematical framework called gauge theory. And in gauge theory, each force is defined by a symmetry group. A specific mathematical object that determines everything about the force: how many charges it has, how many carrier particles it uses, how it behaves at different energies.

The strong force is SU(3). Three charges. Eight carrier particles. 
The weak force is SU(2). Two charges. Three carrier particles.
Electromagnetism is U(1). One charge. One carrier particle.

Three. Two. One.

SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1).

That's the Standard Model. The most precisely tested theory in human history. And for fifty years, physicists have been asking: why 3-2-1? Why not 4-3-2? Why not 5-2-1? What determines those specific numbers?

I think I know. And the answer didn't come from physics.
